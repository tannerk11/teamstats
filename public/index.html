Understanding Advanced Stats

These ratings use an iterative adjustment method inspired by KenPom. Instead of calculating adjustments just once, the system runs 5 rounds of recalculation. Each round, every team's adjusted rating is updated using their opponents' adjusted ratings from the previous round. This creates a chain reaction: beating a team with a tough schedule is worth more than beating a team with an easy schedule, and that "tough schedule" itself accounts for their opponents' schedules, and so on.

NSOS (Net Strength of Schedule)

How tough were your opponents? A positive NSOS means you faced strong teams; negative means easier opponents. The magic of iteration: this uses your opponents' adjusted ratings, which factor in their opponents' strength. So if you played Team A, and Team A played a brutal schedule, your NSOS reflects that — it's "strength of schedule of strength of schedule" baked in automatically.

AdjORTG (Adjusted Offensive Rating)

How good is your offense after accounting for opponent defense quality? Here's where iteration matters: if you scored 80 on Team B, and Team B's defense looks good because they faced great offenses, you get extra credit. Each of the 5 rounds refines this further. By round 5, this number reflects your true offensive ability against an average opponent.

AdjDRTG (Adjusted Defensive Rating)

How good is your defense? Lower is better. If you held Team C to 60 points, and Team C's offense is rated highly because they scored well against tough defenses, your defensive rating improves. The iterative process runs 5 times to ensure the adjustment fully captures the ripple effects of opponent quality throughout the league.

AdjNRTG (Adjusted Net Rating)

The bottom line: AdjORTG minus AdjDRTG. After 5 iterations of refinement, this single number captures a team's true strength. Positive means you're better than average; negative means below average. This is the best stat for comparing any two teams, because it accounts for schedule difficulty at every level — your opponents, their opponents, and beyond.

Why iterate 5 times? Round 1 adjusts for opponent strength. Round 2 adjusts for opponent's opponents. By round 5, the math has settled ("converged") and captures the full chain of schedule effects. Think of it like ripples in a pond — each round lets the ripples spread further until the water is calm. This is the same approach used by KenPom and other trusted rating systems.
